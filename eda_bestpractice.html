Exploratory Analysis Best Practice

tl:dr: This note demonstrates why including best practices in exploratory data analysis improves your ability to understand and make decisions. 

These best practices are applicable to a broad range of analyses and not specific to a team, function or company but provide a lightweight framework for analysing vast amounts of data.
  • The practices include, robust problem definition, structured presentation of findings and peer review.
  • They are not prescriptive rules but following them, even partially makes analysis more consistent and of higher quality.

Why Bother With Best Practices?

 In any data driven organisation or any organisation each piece of analysis aims to drive understanding and decisions.

Stating the obvious, it’s important to get that right. 

At the same time however any additional process creates friction, so one should only add more friction where it adds net positive utility. 

Following best practices serves a few purposes;
  • It helps catch obvious mistakes like using the wrong data sources or omitting key results
  • Reduces both cognitive and statistical bias
  • Grows individuals in problem solving and technical domains
If organizations don’t follow best practices in some form they miss things that should have been caught or avoided, in turn making suboptimal decisions.

For a fraud this could be overstating how much of a risk is on a platform or by not considering the downstream cost (like more false positives) associated with a product solution.

If you have time I’d recommend reading this New Yorker article about how the use of checklists in ICU and aviation has helped saved innumerable lives and millions of dollars, it’s helpful because it drills home the point that qualified and capable people are not immune from basic errors.

Simply put, following agreed best practices reduces oversights and improves the standard of our analysis making the additional friction is worthwhile.
  • These best practices are not supposed to be completely prescriptive however as any analysis is heavily context dependent.
  • The below are guidelines and including them ensures analysis with quality and consistency.

Best Practices 

1. Define The Problem

Analysis should start with defining the problem, stating what you are trying to understand or the hypothesis you’re trying to test.

Without a well structured problem definition, analysis can be directionless. It also makes goal setting and evaluating success more subjective. 

The practice of defining problems leads to a better articulation of the issues at hand, more thoughtfulness in our approach and ultimately better outcomes.

A hypothesis is a definitive explanation of something that can have its validity tested. Including this is an optimal state but not always feasible as initial analysis often consists of ‘understand’ type work. Ideally this would include a critical quantitative threshold that would allow you to accept or reject the proposed hypothesis, allowing us to decide to move forward with changing or building something.

2. Presenting findings

2.1 Assumptions & Trade Offs 

  • We make assumptions in every piece of work we do. 
  • About the data we use, how much it reflects reality and how much historical events predict future outcomes. 
  • We do this both consciously and subconsciously. Being more aware of the assumptions and trade offs made leads us to make more informed decisions.

How does this work in practice?

One commonly made assumption is about the data we use. One might write a query using a particular SQL table and look at a particular time period, say the last three months. 

The assumption implicitly made here is that the table contains accurate information and the historical time period is going to be reflective of what the future will be. 

These are both assumptions. Being aware of them helps us understand the constraints and limitations to our analysis.

A trade off is a compromise made between two incompatible things, for example metrics that move in opposite directions. 

Such as fraud rate and merchant revenue. If a team enforces on more things it reduce rates of fraud but at the same time decrease revenue. 

Including this in analysis is important because when we propose solutions these things are at odds with each other.

You want to be able to ask yourself the following question;

Is this conclusion reasonable given the assumptions and trade offs?

2.2 Key Findings and Next Steps 

The entire point of analysing something is to discover something previously unknown.

A solid analysis highlights this front and centre while also laying out clear next steps.

By doing this, the consumer of your work knows what was discovered, why it matters to them and how it plays a role in the larger collection of problems you are trying to solve.

3. Reference & Peer Review

Referencing and peer review play an important role in analysis.
  • Citing any source that resulted in you reaching your conclusion helps verification and reproducibility. This could be SQL, python notebooks or even news articles. 
  • Analysis peer review allows us to reduce errors and oversight, limits bias and also allows individuals to grow through constructive feedback and inclusion of diverse perspectives.
These practices manifest themselves in different ways for different teams, for example an engineering peer review will be different to a DS review. 

However following them helps collectively make better decisions, helps individuals grow and produces more consistent and higher quality work.
