Exploratory Analysis Best Practice

tl:dr: This note demonstrates why including best practices in exploratory data analysis improves your ability to understand and make decisions. These best practices are applicable to a broad range of analyses and not specific to a team, function or company but provide a lightweight framework for analysing vast amounts of data.

  • The practices include, robust problem definition, structured presentation of findings and peer review. * They are not prescriptive rules but following them, even partially makes analysis more consistent and of higher quality.

    • Why Bother With Best Practices?
    In any data driven organisation or any organisation each piece of analysis aims to drive understanding and decisions.
    Stating the obvious, it’s important to get that right.
    At the same time however any additional process creates friction, so one should only add more friction where it adds net positive utility.
    Following best practices serves a few purposes;
  • It helps catch obvious mistakes like using the wrong data sources or omitting key results
  • Reduces both cognitive and statistical bias
  • Grows individuals in problem solving and technical domains

    • If organizations don’t follow best practices in some form they miss things that should have been caught or avoided, in turn making suboptimal decisions.
    For a fraud this could be overstating how much of a risk is on a platform or by not considering the downstream cost (like more false positives) associated with a product solution.
    If you have time I’d recommend reading this (https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2007/12/10/the-checklist) New Yorker article about how the use of checklists in ICU and aviation has helped saved innumerable lives and millions of dollars, it’s helpful because it drills home the point *that qualified and capable people are not immune from basic errors.
    Simply put, following agreed best practices reduces oversights and improves the standard of our analysis making the additional friction is worthwhile.

  • These best practices are not supposed to be completely prescriptive however as any analysis is heavily context dependent.
  • The below are guidelines and including them ensures analysis with quality and consistency.
    • Best Practices
    1. Define The Problem
    Analysis should start with defining the problem, stating what you are trying to understand or the hypothesis you’re trying to test. Without a well structured problem definition, analysis can be directionless. It also makes goal setting and evaluating success more subjective. The practice of defining problems leads to a better articulation of the issues at hand, more thoughtfulness in our approach and ultimately better outcomes. A hypothesis is a definitive explanation of something that can have its validity tested. Including this is an optimal state but not always feasible as initial analysis often consists of ‘understand’ type work. Ideally this would include a critical quantitative threshold that would allow you to accept or reject the proposed hypothesis, allowing us to decide to move forward with changing or building something.
